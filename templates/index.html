Adaptive Weights

Heritage Action has a floor effect for Democrats — nearly all Dems score 0%, so it adds no information. Its weight drops to 2.7% for Democrats and rises to 17.3% for Republicans where it genuinely discriminates. LCV is the mirror: 13.6% for Democrats, 2.8% for Republicans.

GovTrack confidence scaling — GovTrack's ideology score is derived from bill co-sponsorship patterns, which requires a critical mass of legislative activity to produce a reliable signal. A freshman with 10 bills has far less signal than a veteran with 300. GovTrack's weight is scaled by a sigmoid confidence curve based on each member's total bills introduced + unique cosponsors: near-zero activity (e.g. leadership members who don't sponsor bills, like Pelosi or Jordan) drops GovTrack's weight to ~2%; median activity (~150 actions) gives ~90% of full weight; high-activity members get the full 35%. This prevents a thin co-sponsorship record from pulling a member's composite score away from their voting record.

Visual Encoding

X position → ideology (liberal ← → conservative). Physical distance from center = distance from ideological center. Dot size → Power Index (GovTrack leadership × bills × cosponsors × fundraising × seniority). Opacity → Source Consistency (how much the 5 sources agree). Transparent = sources disagree. Opaque = high confidence placement.

Power Index

Weighted combination of: GovTrack leadership score (25%), bills introduced (20%), unique cosponsors (15%), FEC fundraising (15%), seniority/votes cast (15%), formal role floor — Speaker=100, Leaders=90, Whips=80, Committee Chairs=85 (10%).

When Sources Disagree

Some members will appear further left or right than their voting record alone suggests. This is usually Heritage Action or LCV pulling the composite score — both measure alignment with a specific agenda, not ideology in the abstract.

Example — Brian Fitzpatrick (R-PA): His DW-NOMINATE and GovTrack scores place him right of center (+0.14, −0.03). But Heritage Action rates him only 18% conservative — he's the Problem Solvers Caucus co-chair and one of the most frequent Republican crossover voters. Heritage's 17% weight pulls his composite to −0.08, just left of center. His history chart (Nokken-Poole) tells the truer story: consistently +0.11 to +0.22 across five congresses. When a member's composite and history diverge, the history is usually the cleaner signal — it's based purely on votes, not interest group scorecards.

The opacity of each dot encodes this tension directly: low opacity = sources disagree significantly = treat the placement with more skepticism.
